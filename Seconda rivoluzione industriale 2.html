Wages and Long Hours
Factory workers during the Industrial Revolution were paid very low wages, barely enough to cover rent and food. They worked extremely long hours, often between sixty-five and seventy hours per week, leading to lives filled with hardship, exhaustion, and monotony.

Le condizioni di lavoro

During the Second Industrial Revolution, the introduction of machines caused widespread unemployment as many traditional jobs were replaced by automated processes. Factories became the center of industrial life, but working conditions were often very poor. Workers were forced to work long hours, usually between 12 and 14 hours a day, with little regard for their well-being. Factories were often overcrowded and unhealthy, leading to the spread of diseases due to poor sanitation and a lack of proper living conditions in rapidly growing industrial cities. Life expectancy during this time was very low, around 30 to 33 years, due to poor nutrition and the harsh conditions in which people lived and worked. Overall, the rapid growth of cities and industries contributed to a difficult and often unhealthy way of life for many people.

Nonostante i grandi progressi tecnologici, le condizioni lavorative durante la Seconda Rivoluzione Industriale furono spesso dure e disumane. I lavoratori erano sottoposti a turni lunghi e faticosi, spesso superiori alle 12 ore giornaliere, con pause minime e ambienti di lavoro poco sicuri. Le fabbriche erano caratterizzate da un'aria malsana, piena di polvere, fumo e sostanze chimiche tossiche, che mettevano a rischio la salute dei lavoratori. Nelle industrie pesanti, come le acciaierie e le miniere, gli operai affrontavano rischi ancora maggiori, esposti a temperature estreme, crolli e incidenti con i macchinari.

Overcrowded and Polluted Living Conditions
Workers lived in hastily constructed towns near factories, known as "mushroom towns." These towns lacked essential services like clean water, proper sanitation, and open spaces. The air and water were heavily polluted, and houses were small and overcrowded, contributing to poor health and quality of life.

Inoltre, il lavoro minorile era ampiamente diffuso: molti bambini lavoravano per aiutare le famiglie, spesso in condizioni peggiori rispetto agli adulti. La frammentazione del lavoro in mansioni semplici e ripetitive, introdotta con il Taylorismo e perfezionata con la catena di montaggio, rese i lavoratori anonimi e facilmente sostituibili. Questa "deumanizzazione" del lavoro portò a un senso di alienazione tra gli operai, che si sentivano ridotti a semplici ingranaggi di una macchina.La crescente consapevolezza di queste condizioni portò, negli anni successivi, all'organizzazione di sindacati e movimenti operai, che iniziarono a lottare per migliori condizioni di lavoro, salari più equi e la riduzione dell'orario lavorativo. Questo fu un passaggio fondamentale per l'evoluzione dei diritti dei lavoratori nel XX secolo.




Child Labor in Factories and Mines
Many working-class families depended on their children's income to survive. Factory owners preferred to employ children because they could be paid much less, were easier to control, and could perform tasks requiring small hands, like repairing machinery or crawling through narrow mine tunnels. This work was extremely dangerous and contributed to the low life expectancy of the time.
